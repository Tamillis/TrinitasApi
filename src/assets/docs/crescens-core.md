# Crescens: Core

Crescens is a tabletop roleplaying game (if you're here, you know what that is).

It has been design with love from both OSR and new schools of thought - aiming for both narrative and tactical play.

Crescens is designed to be setting agnostic. The core rules here describe characters and what they can do, with core examples of systems designed to be expanded in modules that best enable the setting and theme they're for.

## Decision Making

### The Roll
When you make a roll, you roll the relevant Statistic die and add the relevant Skill's Rank, and if you have Injuries, detract the most relevant one (if any). 

Compare the result to the Target Number to see if you succeed or not. The Target Number is set by a facet of the game, like another character's Defence, or by the GM (see below).

> Statistic die + Skill rank - Injury rank vs TN

A result of 1 on the die is always a failure. Failure is always a risk for anyone. See TN's for success values. Only the adept are capable of reaching higher values at all, but frequent steps up and down the chain are intended, through Powers and Advantages & Disadvantages - which player characters are encouraged to look for.

### The Dice Chain
Crescens uses a chain of dice to represent capability going from d2 to d20. Every step of the chain is:

> d2 - d3 - d4 - d6 - d8 - d10 - d12 - d16 - d20

#### Rolling unusual dice
A d2 can be "rolled" with a coin (heads 2, tails 1), or with a regular d6 with 1-2-3 being 1 and 4-5-6 being 2. A d3 can also be rolled with a regular d6: 1-2 for 1, 3-4 for 2 and 5-6 for 3.

A d16 is a bit tricky to roll but can be simulated with a d8 and a "double-it" die: any other die. If the Double-It die is above it's average (so a 4-5-6 on a d6), add 8 to the first d8 for your result, otherwise just use the initial d8 result.

### Target Numbers (TN)

A Target Number represents the difficulty of a task - the value that must be met or bested for a "success". The GM sets this based on the fiction, using the following as a guide:

| Difficulty | Target Number |
|---|---|
| Medium | 4 |
| Difficult | 6 |
| Hard | 8 |
| Extreme | 12 |
| Legendary | 16 |
| Mythic | 20 |

Note: there is no "easy" category. Such activity should not be considered for a roll.

A character with a d6 Statistic and rank 2 in the relevant skill has a maximum possible roll of 8 and an average of 5.5, meaning Hard tasks are barely in reach without help or exceptional circumstances. This is intentional. Most people need help or to manipulate their situation to overcome unusual odds. This is where Advantages and Disadvantages come in, which players are encouraged to seek out.

Conversely highly skilled and/or high stat characters could *always* succeed on Medium, Difficult or even Hard tasks, as such exemplars should.

#### Opposed Rolls
Opposed actions, where two characters work against each other directly, do not use a TN. They are resolved as **Opposed Rolls**: both roll and add relevant skills, and the higher result wins. Ties go to the character who initiated the action, unless the GM rules otherwise.

### Advantages and Disadvantages

When circumstances favour a roll, the GM or a Power may grant an Advantage: a step the statistic die up one size on the chain for that roll.

Similarly, when circumstances hinder a roll, a Disadvantage can apply: step the statistic die down one size.

> For example, if Gronan the Barbarian is kicking down a reinforced door, the GM may say that is a Difficult Task (TN 6), and Gronan uses his Strength (d8) and Martial Arts skill (+2). Gronan has a 50 / 50 chance of opening the door. If his ally Torry Snark, who has a high Crafting skill, helps by pointing out the door's structural weak points, the GM could grant Gronan an Advantage, making his roll d10+2. Better odds!

Multiple sources of Advantage and Disadvantage stack, stepping the die further up or down the chain. A character with two sources of Advantage on a d4 roll uses a d8. 

Advantage and Disadvantage from multiple sources cancel each other out.

If you reach the end of the chain (d2 / d20), stop.

## Character Statistics

Statistics represent the broad physical and mental dimensions of a character. Any challenge can be approached with any Statistic and any Skill, so long as the Statistic and Skill match the action the character is attempting.

> A silver-tongued negotiator and an iron-fisted bruiser might both use their Persuasion Skill to get what they want; one with Intelligence and the other with Strength. Two characters attempting to leap across a chasm may use Agility, but one with their Wilderness skill in navigating wild places, and the other with Crafting, throwing together a pole to help them vault with.

The 5 statistics are:

| Statistic | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Strength | Raw physical power: lifting, forcing, breaking, enduring, and hitting hard, and representing the physical capacity to absorb punishment. |
| Agility | Speed, precision, and physical grace: moving well, striking accurately, performing with your body, and reacting quickly. |
| Perception | Awareness, both physical and social: noticing things, reading situations, sensing what others miss, and reacting to the world as it actually is rather than as you expect it to be. |
| Intelligence | The processing and recall of information: scholarship, cunning, careful analysis, and working things out. |
| Spirit | Spirit is will, connection, and inner force. It is the act of resisting, persevering, and relating to others. Most importantly, it is the ability to engage with things beyond the mundane. It also represents the will to keep going when the body wants to stop.

### Statistic Dice
A character's Statistics range from d4 to d12 (d4 to d8 in character-creation), and characters can temporarily or permanently increase or decrease their Stats along the chain as well - such as through Encumbrance reducing their Agility, or Stat Increase during progression increasing a statistic 1 step.

A d4 is considered "poor" capability, a d6 "average", a d8 "talented", a d10 "exceptional" and a d12 "genius".

## Skills

Skills are trained capabilities in fields of expertise that your character has learned to do. They are from Rank 0 to Rank 6. A Skill rank adds directly to your roll when you are using that Skill.

A skill rank of 0 means **Untrained** - you can attempt any action where the GM asks for a specific skill. 

An **Unskilled roll** is where you roll your statistic die alone, bringing no particular expertise to a task. 

A skill and statistic pairings are not fixed. The same skill might be rolled against different statistics depending on how the character approaches a task. The table decide together which statistic fits the approach taken.

### The Skill List

The following are the core skills of Crescens. They are a starting point, players, GMs and Modules add to and change this list. 

- **Animal Handling** - understanding and being understood by animals, and riding them.
- **Art** - playing music, singing, performing, painting, and other creative expression.
- **Charm** - proper etiquette, pleasing performance, and social grace.
- **Crafting** - the making of objects, from tools to fine work.
- **Deception** - lying, applying disguises, and impersonating others convincingly.
- **First Aid** - practical and prompt treatment of injuries and ailments.
- **Insight** - reading people, discerning lies, and understanding motives.
- **Intimidation** - browbeating, threatening, and projecting menace.
- **Knowledge** - a specific field of learning. When taking this skill, choose one field. Knowledge may be taken multiple times for different fields. Examples include:
  - Science
  - Culture
  - Engineering
  - History
  - Medicine
  - Nature
  - Occult
  - Religion. 
- **Logic** - applying reasoned thinking, strategy, tactics, and the piecing together of evidence.
- **Persuasion** - the art of turning people around to your point of view.
- **Piloting** - driving vehicles, sailing watercraft, and navigating by map.
- **Search** - active investigation, spotting hidden things, and researching texts.
- **Stealth** - hiding, moving unnoticed, and constructing camouflage.
- **Subterfuge** - forgery, lock-picking, spying, and other quieter criminal arts.
- **Wilderness** - surviving and navigating wild places, tracking, finding food and shelter, and engaging with the natural world.

Crescencs Core allows for three broad fighting skills:
- **Weaponry** - melee weapons from knives to batons to full-on swords
- **Shooting** - ranged weaponry from archery to modern firearms
- **Martial Arts** - unarmed combat techniques

If combat is a large part of your table's game, consider using a combat-focused module like `Swordplay`, which replaces these Skills with more granular weapon skills, and much more!

#### Custom Skills
Players may define their own custom skills, following the same principle: a single evocative phrase describing a specific area of capability. 

> Do not create skills that are a superset of a Statistic, like "athletics" as a superset of Strength, as these are not the fields-of-expertise a Skill is supposed to be, and prevents the free association of Statistics and Skills.

## Injuries

When a character is hurt, they acquire Injuries. Injuries work like negative skills, detracting from rolls. The Rank of an Injury is set by the damage that caused it.

Injuries are descriptions and locations for a kind of wound that applies a minus modifier to a roll. If a character has multiple Injuries at various Ranks, use the highest most relevant.

> Velma has a rank 2 'Gashed Leg' and a rank 1 'Bruised Knuckles', for example. If she were to scale a wall, either could hinder, so she applies a -2 penalty to the roll for the higher of the two ranks.

Injuries accumulate, and enough of them will take a character to their Knock Out Limit, removing them from the fight entirely until they Recover. See `Combat` rules for the full Injury and Recovery system.

## Design Philosophy
This section contains no rules, just the thought behind some of them. Feel free to go to the next page if you're looking for further rules.

Three ideas sit at the heart of Crescens.

**Vectors of approach** - A player describes *what* a character attempts to do, and the mechanics of the game - the Statistics, Skills and Powers - describe *how*. The system is purposefully inteded to not pigeon-hole actions, but simple enable actions and generate the narrative behind them. For a character that wants to *intimidate* - a character could use their Intimidate skill to browbeat someone through physical menace with Strength. But they could also intimidate someone through Subterfuge and Intelligence quietly revealing blackmail they know. A character could intimidate through dexterous acrobatics showing up another, or through sheer overbearing oration with their spirit and persuasion. The fiction leads the mechanics.

**Verisimilitude** The rules generally apply descriptors rather than numbers, trying to create scenarios better able to reflect a lived reality. Damage leaves specific marks on specific people through Injuries, instead of HP. A character is hurt in ways that matter to what they do next, not in ways that merely subtract from a number until the number reaches zero. Skills represent learnable things and are open ended. Every Statistic is intended to be broad enough to apply to any skill.

**Impact** Crescens is not a game where the heroes are invulnerable. Violence has consequences. Going into a fight unprepared or badly outmatched, or just letting it go on too long, is dangerous. Injuries will start to drag as each wound slows you down, until either you or your enemy are Knocked Out.

### When Not to Roll
Rolling is only intended to be called for when needed, and not to be a barrier to story or conversely a player tool to force a GM's hand.

A roll is called for when failure is plausible and consequential. A character picking a complex lock under fire with inadequate tools rolls, but one with the right tools and all the time in the world doesn't: even for a difficult lock. Now if the latter character had to pick that lock in the next 30 seconds because the guard is coming, then you have consequences and a roll should be made to see if they pick it in time.

In other words: rolling dice is for moments of genuine uncertainty with meaningful consequences on either side.

- If a character could not reasonably fail, do not roll.
- If failure would produce nothing interesting, do not roll.
- If the only consequence of a roll would be to slow things down, do not roll.
- If the GM does not call for a roll, do not roll.

The GM should always ask: what happens if this fails? If the answer is nothing of consequence, skip the roll.

### Social Skills and Statistics

Social interaction is not a single statistic. Different approaches to dealing with people draw on genuinely different capabilities, and the statistic used should reflect what the character is actually doing.

The following examples use core skills, but the same logic applies to custom skills:

- **Entertain** with Art or Charm and Agility - a physical performance, dexterous musicianship, or a carefully choreographed display that puts an audience at ease.
- **Impress** with Charm and Spirit or Logic and Intelligence - a display of presence and bearing, or a compelling and well-reasoned argument, that earns respect and favourable attention.
- **Deceive** with Deception and Intelligence - constructing and maintaining a convincing falsehood, from a simple lie to an elaborate false identity.
- **Intimidate** with Intimidation and Strength - the threat of physical consequence, projecting menace through presence and capability. Alternatively, with Knowledge and Intelligence - the quiet revelation that you know something they would rather you did not.
- **Convince** with Persuasion and Intelligence - arguing a case, appealing to reason, or making an emotional appeal through carefully chosen words. Alternatively, with Art and Agility a moving performance that shifts hearts rather than minds.
- **Fathom** with Insight and Perception - reading a person's state of mind, discerning whether they are lying, and understanding what they actually want beneath what they say.
