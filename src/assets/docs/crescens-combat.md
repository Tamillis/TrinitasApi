# Crescens Combat

Combat in Crescens is meant to feel dramatic, dangerous, and snappy. Characters feel each blow thanks to the Injuries system, adding drama and danger: and Death is always a possibility, but distinct from being Knocked Out of the fight. One can be Out but not Unconscious, Dying but not Out, and Unconscious without necessarily Dying - and every fight has the possibility of consequences thanks to Lasting Injuries. One Action Rounds mean combat is a fast and fluid affair at the table where each character takes only one action and players are constantly engaged with what is happening and each other.

## Rounds

Combat is played out in **Rounds**, of roughly five seconds. Every character in a Round has one **Action**. If a character has access to any, a character can also take one **Bonus Action** per Round. 

All characters act simultaneously and there is no turn-order based initiative system. Players resolve action dynamically at the table by resolving **Engagements**.

### How a Round Works

At the start of each Round:

1. The GM declares the intentions and targets of all enemies in the scene. Some special enemies may have **Hidden Actions** (see below) but most opponents telegraph what they are about to do, just as their body language and positioning would in reality: letting Players decide how to act.
2. Players discuss among themselves and declare their Actions: thus determining Engagements (see below).
3. Resolve the Engagements in order of whichever players are ready and willing.
4. Resolve un-engaged actions (such as Ranged Attacks).
5. Resolve Movement

Within the fiction, this is all happening effectively 'at once'. When you think about it, most Actions can resolve simultaneously. Two characters swinging at each other do so, both potentially land hits, and both potentially collapse at the same moment - the GM narrates how one's hammer blow connects just as a dagger finds the gap in their armour.

### Hidden Enemy Actions

Powerful or well-trained enemies may take **Hidden Actions**, their intentions kept secret until players have committed to their own Actions. The GM notes the Hidden Action privately. 

Players may attempt to **Read** a Hidden Action as their Action, making a Perception check against a TN set by the enemy's Stat and Skill in use. On a success, the Hidden Action is revealed to all other players before they finalise their decisions.

## Actions

- **Attack** - you Attack a character in reach / range of your weapon. Make an 'Attack Roll' vs their Evasion to see if you Hit. If multiple targets are part of the attack, make a Roll per enemy. Use the Statistic that powers the attack (typically Strength, sometimes Agility), and the Skill relevant to your weapon (Weaponry, Firearms or Martial Arts).
- **Defend** - by blocking, dodging or some other Skill you detail, you replace your Evasion with a roll: the Stat and Skill of your defensive action. Any Attack against you this Round uses this value, or keeping your Evasion if it is higher, as the TN. For example: a character using pocket-sand could use Agility + Subterfuge. A character reading and anticipating attacks could use Perception + Insight.
- **Move** - as an Action, you move your Speed along a path of your choice towards a target a distance of 30ft.
- **Discern** - as an Action, you can make a Perception check against one enemy whose has a Hidden Action. If successful, their Action is no longer hidden and everyone in the combat can know.
- **Assist** - as an Action, you aid an ally in attacking an enemy within your reach. You feint, distract a target, or in some other way team up to make your ally's attack more effective. If your ally Attacks the target this Round, the Attack is made with an Advantage.
- **Hide** - as an Action, you make a Stealth check in an attempt to hide from those you are obscured from, versus a TN of their average Perception. If you succeed, you gain the benefits of being Hidden: Attacks against you have a Disadvantage, and your Attack have an Advantage. When you make an Attack, you are no longer Hidden. If you or others Move such that you are no longer obscured, you are no longer Hidden from them.
- **Interact** - as an Action, you interact, a short series of motions that achieves a simple goal, with something or someone. This includes: giving an unconscious ally a healing tincture, arranging the runes of a magical interface, frisking a character for weapons, pulling the chains of a pulley, finding a particular page of a book, or creating a rough sketch in the sand.
- **Grapple** - as an Action you attempt an Opposed Roll against a target to grab hold of them. If you succeed the target Character is Immobilised. On subsequent Rounds you can maintain the grapple (no additional roll needed) as your Action, otherwise ending the grapple. If you maintain the grapple, you can use your Bonus Action to Move yourself and the enemy as if going through Difficult Terrain, make yourself and the enemy Prone, or make an unarmed attack against the grappled character. The grappled character can use their Action to attempt to escape the Grapple, making another Opposed Roll with you, ending the grapple on a success. The Immobilised condition otherwise applies as normal. GMs may add that such actions have a Disadvantage for being grappled.
- **Shove** - as an Action you attempt to make an Enemy Prone or move to a location of your choice in Melee range with an Opposed Roll.
- **Stabilise** - take an Action to see to a Fatal Injury (yours or another's within Melee range) using the First Aid Skill vs a TN of the rank of the injury + the number of fatal injuries they have. On a success, it is no longer Fatal Injury.

### Bonus Actions

Some Powers and mechanics provide **Bonus Actions**: quick additional effects a character can perform alongside their main Action without hindering it. When your character uses a Bonus Action, narrate how your your character weaves it into their Action. Instead of an Action and Bonus Action, you can also do 2 Bonus Actions in a Round.

> "I *Stand* (a Bonus Action), swinging my baton for his shins in retaliation (an Attack Action)"

- **Go Prone / Stand** - as a Bonus Action you make yourself Prone. Prone makes being shot at occur at a Disadvantage. Going from Prone to standing takes a Stand Bonus Action.
- **Quick Use** - as a Bonus Action, you make a single motion to use an item or device. This includes: pulling a lever, pushing a button, drinking a concoction readily available, sending a signal (hand, flag, flare etc.), or unlocking a chest with a readily available key.

## Engagements

**Engagements** are momentary pairings or small groups that form in any real fight, allowing players to resolve simultaneous combat easily, pairing by pairing.

An **Engagement** is when a character attacks an enemy in their reach who is attacking them.

If an attacked character wants to attack a third character, they may do so, but are considered to be **Flanked** by the enemy they've ignored - meaning they gain an Advantage to strike their target. 

Typically a character will face those attacking them, creating these naturally occuring engagements.

Conga-line engagements, where A is attacking B who is attacking C who is attacking D etc. are discouraged.

Engagements can then be resolved in order of which Player Engagements know what they want to do first, the results of which will often help other player's resolve their own engagements.

As noted, non-engaged character's actions are resolved after all ongoing engagements (i.e., melee before ranged & ancilliary).

## Attacks

### Attacker's Intent

When you take an Attack Action, you also declare your **Attacker's Intent**: a specific statement of what you are trying to do to harm your target and where you are aiming.

> "I swing for his sword hand. He'll never hold a blade again."
> "I drive the point at her throat, interrupting her speech."
> "I try to sever the machine's wiring - I want to disabled it without wrecking it."

Intent determines what the damage result means, and helps GMs adjucated Advantages and Disadvantages. This intent is used to decide any resulting Injury descriptors.

If Attacker's Intent should make an Attack easier, the GM should grant an Advantage. If it is harder (such as a specific limb), an Disadvantage. If it is far harder (such as a bullseye), the GM should add 2 Disadvantages.

When you roll to deal damage you use Intent to interpret the descriptor and location.

### Rolling to Hit

**Attack Rolls** are where you make a roll using the relevant **Skill** (Weaponry, Shooting or Martial Arts) and the appropriate **Statistic** (usually Strength for melee, Agility for ranged) against the target's **Evasion** as the TN. On a success, you Hit. You then Roll Damage.

### Rolling Damage & Armour
Each weapon has a Damage type and Damage value expressed as a die and sometimes +/- some value. For example, Fists:

> Fists - d2 Bludgeoning Damage

If the target has **Armour**, reduce the damage by that Armour's **Armour Value** to a minimum of 1. 

Armour makes hits hurt less. Armour values run from 1 to 6 with specific values detailed in the equipment module you use (if any).

The final damage value is the Rank of the Injury caused. If it makes more sense to increase an existing Injury's Rank by that amount, do so. This cannot increase an injury beyond Rank 6.

Reference the Damage table for that Damage Type for ideas for Injury descriptors.

### Targeting Unarmoured Locations

Armour covers specific parts of the body. Attacker's Intent that targets an unarmoured location ignores the armour reduction entirely. 

Intent can and should matter tactically as well as narratively.

## Range

Positioning for Attacks, distances covered in a Move and other interactions fall into bands. Different modules may use differing bands if that better reflects the equipment and features of the module.
There are four broad range bands characters exist within, centring on the scene.

| Band | Distance | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Melee | <= 15ft / 5 metres | Within 'reach' of one-another, touch and lunge. |
| Short | 30ft / 10 metres | The diagonal of a large room |
| Medium | 100ft / 30 metres | The height of a tall tree |
| Long | 300ft / 100 metres | The length of a pitch |
| Extreme | > 300ft / 100 metres | Anything longer than Long |

## Injuries

Injuries represent damage and function exactly like negative Skills.

An Injury has two components:
- A *descriptor* - what kind of wound it is, generated from the fiction and Intent, often including location
- A *rank* - how severe it is, from 1 to 6 like any other skill

Characters gain an **Injury** when they take Damage at a rank equal to that Damage, or an existing Injury is made worse by a number of Ranks equal to the Damage.

The rank of an Injury applies as a negative modifier to any roll where that Injury is meaningfully relevant. A rank 3 Gashed Sword Arm applies -3 to any roll involving that arm. A rank 2 Fractured Rib applies -2 to any physically demanding movement. The GM and player interpret relevance together.

If more than one injury could apply to a roll, use the highest rank (just like a Skill).

Injuries accumulate. A character carrying several serious Injuries will find themselves increasingly unable to function effectively across many kinds of actions.

If the total ranks of all Injuries reach your character's Limit - they are Knocked Out.

## Knocked Out

A character's **Knock Out Limit**, a.k.a. simply **Limit**, is the total negative Injury rank they can bear before becoming unable to act. It is equal to the maximum face value of their Strength die plus the maximum face value of their Spirit die; known as Strength Max and Spirit Max.

When the total rank of all a character's Injuries meets or exceeds their Knock Out Limit, they are **Knocked Out**. They collapse and cannot take Actions. 

### Unconsciouss
Knocked Out characters are not necessarily Unconscious; they are simply out of the fight. The GM or player may describe a Knock Out as most thematically valid as being Unconsciouss, otherwise characters are free to slowly crawl and mock through gritted teeth, or take whatever other non-participatory action they wish.

Other Powers and effects may cause a character to become Unconsciouss - unable to participate in the fight anymore without necessarily being at or over their limit, or dying.

## Fatal Injuries and Death Rolls 

When a character suffers an Injury of rank 4 or greater, wounds that could kill if left unaddressed, the GM should consider marking it as **Fatal**.

> An arterial cut to the throat, a transfixion through the chest, bones shattered in a critical location.

At the end of a Round, for each Fatal Injury the character makes a **Death Roll**.

> **Death Roll** = Spirit Roll vs a TN of the Rank of the Fatal Injury + the number of Fatal Injuries they have (including this one). 

If they have 1 rank 4 Fatal Injury, the TN is 5. If they have a rank 5 Fatal Injury, and 3 Fatal Injuries in total, that Death TN is 8. A roll is made for each Fatal Injury at the end of a Round.

On a success, nothing happens. On a failure, that Fata Injury increases Rank by 1.

If a Fatal Injury reaches Rank 7 (an impossible rank), the character dies. The GM may decide they have some time to say last words, or they may die instantly.

### Dying
Having at least one Fatal Injury means the character is **Dying** - i.e. making Death Rolls. 

As such, it is possible for a character to be Dying without being Knocked Out, or Knocked Out without Dying. This also does not necessarily correspond to being Unconscious either.

A Dying character may still act, still fight, still drag themselves toward the door - but they are on a clock, and the clock is the wound itself getting worse.

### Lasting Injuries
When a character is Knocked Out, they gain a Lasting Injury. Roll on the table below after a fight has finished (only once, no matter how many times they get Knocked Out).

Roll a d20.

| d20 | Wound |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Dramatic Death** - you have taken a sudden and lethal wound, but fate gives you one last moment. You have one final Action at the end of the Round, after which you die. |
| 2-3 | **Facial Injury** - roll on the Facial Injuries table below. If you roll the same Facial Injury twice, you suffer a Dramatic Death instead. |
| 4 | **Facial Disfigurement** - your face is visibly and permanently damaged. Any roll relying on appearance is made with a Disadvantage. Further Facial Disfigurement becomes a Facial Injury instead. |
| 5 | **Lose an Arm** - your arm is gone above the elbow. You cannot hold more than one item until the limb is replaced. Rolled twice, both arms are lost. |
| 6 | **Lose a Leg** - your leg is gone above the knee. Your movement is halved until the limb is replaced. Rolled twice, both legs are lost. |
| 7-8 | **Chest wound** - you take a chest wound, an internal injury. You take a permanent Chest Wound Injury at Rank I that cannot be healed or recovered without a Full Recovery, where the injury transforms to Heavy Scarring. You can suffer this injury repeatedly, increasing the Rank each time. |
| 9-10 | **Scarring** - severe internal or external damage leaves a permanent mark. The GM gains one Disadvantage token per session to spend against you when your injuries flare. |
| 11+ | **Lucky Break** - some light scarring may cross your body, but you otherwise luckily suffer no lasting injury from this Knock Out. |

## Death

Death in Crescens comes from injuries becoming too great to bear. There are three main causes of death:

- A **rank 6 single Injury** - if a character takes a Rank 6 Injury in one go, this Kills them instantly. The GM may grant them a Dramatic Death (see Lasting Injuries result 1).
- Any Fatal Injury accumulates to Rank "7" - as this is impossible.
- An Instant Death effect - typically only possible due to very dangerous situations. If a character hits the ground at terminal velocity, they most likely are simply dead.

## Recovery

### Per Fight

Some Powers and mechanics recover **Per Fight** - they refresh at the end of a combat encounter, representing reserves of energy and focus that replenish with a narrative moment to catch your breath once the danger has passed.

### Rest

A **Rest** is a full night's sleep or equivalent period of genuine recuperation, typically eight hours. At the end of a Rest:

- Characters may make a Recovery Roll .
- Characters clear the effects of most temporary conditions.
- Many Powers and Module rules also key off of "Per Rest".

Characters who go without a Rest accumulate **Exhaustion** Injuries at the GM's discretion. The specific rank and pace depends on the circumstances; a forced march is different from a sleepless night of study. Typically 1 Rank of Exhaustion Injury per "exhausting" activity: 1 rank from a forced march, 1 rank from carrying a wounded ally through the night, etc.

### Recovering Injuries
Injuries recover slowly and imperfectly, healing night-by-night before it stops affecting the character.

**Recovery Roll** - at the end of a Rest, a character makes a Strength or Spirit (their choice) roll with no Skill modifier. They reduce a number of Injuries equal to the result by 1 Rank. This is also true for Knocked Out characters, which may bring their Injury total below their Limit and remove the Knocked Out condition. If the recovery roll is more than the number of Injuries, the "extra" is ignored.

> For example, Tory has 3 Injuries of ranks 1, 2 and 3. They roll recovery and get a 5. They recover all 3 Injuries by 1 rank, becoming two injuries of ranks 1 and 2. 

## Movement
One Move covers a distance of 30ft - i.e. Short range.

Movement is declared alongside all other Actions at the start of a Round but resolved last, after all other Actions and Engagements.

When declaring a Move, declare also the target of the movement - the character or location you are moving toward or away from. When multiple characters are moving relative to each other, their declared targets usually make the outcome clear without needing to track precise positioning.

When the outcome of simultaneous movement is unclear, make Opposed Agility rolls to determine who gets where first.

### Difficult Terrain

Moving through difficult terrain costs double movement. Climbing or swimming costs quadruple. Impassable terrain must be circumnavigated.

## Conditions

**Blinded** - the character cannot see. They automatically fail any roll requiring sight, and attacks relying on sight are made at two Disadvantages.

**Charmed** - the character regards the source of the Charm as an ally and will act accordingly, though will not act against their own fundamental interests.

**Deafened** - the character cannot hear. They automatically fail any roll requiring hearing.

**Dying** - the character has a Fatal Injury that is ticking up each Round. See Fatal Injuries.

**Exhausted (rank)** - treated as an Injury of the listed rank, applied to all physically and mentally demanding rolls.

**Flanking** - when you are the character in an Engagement who the enemy is not primarily targeting, you have an Advantage on your attack rolls against them.

**Frightened** - the character has a Disadvantage on all rolls while the source of their fear is in sight, and must move away from it if they choose to move.

**Hidden** - attacks against the character have a Disadvantage. Their own attacks against those they are Hidden from have an Advantage. Taking an Attack Action or any similarly conspicuous action ends the condition after the action is finished.

**Immobilised** - the character cannot take the Move action. Other Actions may have a Disadvantage.

**Knocked Out** - the character's total Injury ranks have met or exceeded their Knock Out Limit - they cannot take any Actions.

**Pained** - the character must succeed on a Spirit roll against a GM-set TN at the start of their Action or lose it entirely.

**Poisoned** - the character has a Disadvantage on all attack rolls and physical skill rolls.

**Prone** - the character is on the ground. Their movement is quartered. Attack rolls against them have an Advantage if the attacker is in reach, or a Disadvantage if ranged. Attacks they make have a Disadvantage.

**Sluggish** - the character may take an Action or a Bonus Action this Round, not both.

**Stunned** - the character is Incapacitated for one Round ad unable to take an Action.

**Unconscious** - the character is unaware of their surroundings, drops whatever they are holding, falls Prone, and automatically fails any roll requiring awareness or physical response.

**Unstable** - the character has a Disadvantage on attacks. Typically caused by riding a mount or standing on a rolling ship's deck.
