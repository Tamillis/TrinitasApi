# Game Master's Guide
This is a document to help you, the Game Master, run a game of Crescens. This games masters guide will not include the general advice that has already been written a hundred times and better across many blogs, videos and essays on the internet and elsewhere for running a TTRPG, planning session, curating a scene and creating a world. This games masters guide will contain the information that you need to run a game of Crescens and any other areas of games mastering where the mechanics of Crescens make themselves known.

## Running the Game
The GM's job is to facilitate the story being told. You are the stage, the props, and all the supporting cast. You frame scenes, call for rolls, and keep things moving. You are running a world that reacts honestly to their choices. Together with your Players, you make up The Table.

### The Table
The Table is Crescens short-hand for the Game Master and the Players, the people at the table, who're telling a story together. It is the group perspective, which Crescens relies on to build a consensus on what is happening in-fiction. Core Mechanics rely on The Table's understanding: the meaning and application of Stat and Skill pairings, the kind of Injuries that should result from an Attacker's Intent, what custom skills cover, etc.

When something at the table feels unclear or contested, that is a conversation to have with the table.

### The GM's Role
What the GM is actually doing at the table.

- Reacting to the actions of the players as the stage on which they play
- Playing NPCs and enemies with individual intent, to bring the story to life.
- Keeping up the pace by calling for rolls only when failure is plausible and the consequence is meaningful, and stepping in when individual Players feel the need to initiate rolls or slow down play against the will of the table.
- Rewarding player creativity and story involvement with Advantages, and maintaining versimilitude by mediating more out-there decisions with Disadvantages and other reactions.

### When to Roll
A roll is called for when failure is plausible and consequential. If a character could not reasonably fail, then they don't. Similarly, if failure would produce nothing interesting, do not roll. If failure is inevitable, don't roll. Just narrate the stage.

Always ask: what happens if this fails?

Note that TNs start at 4 (Medium), so if a task would below that ("Easy"), don't roll.

## Adjudication

The GM has two main tools for translating all player (and NPC) action into dice rolls to be impartially decided: the Target Number and the Advantage/Disadvantage system. 

They're two different tools intended to be used for two different needs:

### Target Numbers
The TN represents the baseline difficulty of a task, set by the thing being attempted, in typical circumstances. A locked chest is TN 6 (if 6 is what you go for) whether a master thief or a nervous farmer is picking it. It doesn't vary for the character.

For reference, here is the Core TN table again, with some added guidance:

| Difficulty | Target Number | Description |
|---|---| --- |
| Medium | 4 | Tasks that your average fellow would succeed at most of the time. |
| Difficult | 6 | Tasks that your average fellow would fail at most of the time. |
| Hard | 8 | Tasks that require some Advantage to overcome for your average fellow, or someone well trained. |
| Extreme | 12 | Tasks that require significant teamwork or exceptional talent and skill to overcome. Even a Genius (stat d12) if untrained would struggle to overcome such a challenge. |
| Legendary | 16 | Tasks that only legends could beat with any regularity. |
| Mythic | 20 | Only those graced by Gods or superhuman capability could overcome such tasks. Do not use lightly. |

> An "Average Fellow" is probably rolling an average Stat (d6) with a +1 or +2, assuming they can apply, in some way, one of their skills. That gives an average of 4.5 / 5.5.
>
> "Well trained" is a skill rank of 3+, and above average stats are d8, giving a "capable" individual an average of 7.5. More often-than-not, they'd still fail "Hard" tasks.

### Advantage and Disadvantage
Advantages and Disadvantages are the GM's response to how a player character approaches a task. If a character seeks good positioning, makes preparation, brings the right tools, adapts to the environment, or applies some other creative thinking, you should reward the player with an Advantage to step the die up. Up to 3 Advantages at once is a good general rule of thumb for a character that has gone well out of their way to improve their odds.

As each Advantage or Disadvantage steps the relevant stat die one step up or down the dice chain, and they stack, it is intended for players to seek them out, and for GM's to dole out Disadvantages for poor decisions, unfortunate circumstance or transient difficulty. 

The TN's and the maths behind stat die and skill ranks are meant to work within this system, particularly at higher TNs or when attempting something outside their strengths. GMs should not keep the bar for them too high. And the converse is also true, don't be afraid to lay out Disadvantages, giving quick, tangible and impactful consequences to player actions.

> Imagine; a character attempts to scale a rain-slicked wall at night. The GM sets TN 4 for the climb (climbing a person-high wall is a Medium difficulty for this particular table), but says the dark and the rain are two Disadvantages. The character's ally stands below the wall and braces their feet and cups their hands to boost, earning an Advantage. The character runs and jumps, using their Strength (d6) to rush the wall, and relying on their skill in Subterfuge (2). The Advantage and Disadvantage cancel, leaving the final roll as d4 (1 Disadvantage) + 2. They roll a 2, getting 4 total, and successfully climbing the wall. They turn around to pull up their ally, which the GM says is Easy as they can take the time thanks to the cover of night, and they carry on with their adventure.

> A character is trying to fast-talk a city guard into letting them through a checkpoint, which the GM says is TN 6. They have forged papers prepared in advance thanks to earlier escapes, a powerful boon, which the GM says is 2 Advantages, but they also speak with a noticeable foreign accent, earning a Disadvantage. This grants a net Advantage, stepping their stat die up once.

## NPCs

Non-player characters are the characters your players meet, and you will need to both prepare them and make them up on the fly. Crescens presents 4 tiers of NPCs creation tools to help you do so. Each tier builds up into the next.

### Background NPCs
Use background NPCs to give texture to the world, ones that are present for an interaction or two.

They're quick and mechanically simple. They have no stats. They have a face and enough personality to feel real for the moments they occupy.

Generate one on the fly with four d6 rolls:

| d6 | Occupation | Disposition | Personality | Quirk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Labourer | Hostile | Nervous | Owes a dangerous debt |
| 2 | Trader | Suspicious | Boastful | Wants out of this town |
| 3 | Official | Indifferent | Warm | Haunted by a secret about something / someone |
| 4 | Criminal | Curious | Blunt | Deeply superstitious |
| 5 | Servant | Friendly | Comic | Searching for something / someone |
| 6 | Soldier | Desperate | Dark | In disguise / faking it |

### Social NPCs
Use social NPCs when players make meaningful interactions that require rolls.

It's best if they have a motivation to drive the interaction: the goal that they want out of it.

Generate a Social NPC by setting their **Primary** and **Secondary** statistics (of the normal 5 stats), and one to three skills of ranks 1-4 (whatever feels right).

Use only those in their rolls (for simplicity). This keeps them focused, characterising and easy to run. If you need a different stat, just use a d4 / d6 for less / more impactful characters.

For example:

> *Lucy the Bard* - trader, curious, comic, haunted by a secret
>
> Wants an audience for her talents and a warm meal tonight. Wants the players to hire her.
>
> Spirit d8, Agility d6, d4 | Art 2, Charm 3, Insight 1

### Combat NPCs
Use combat NPCs when fighting with or against them.

They're not as complex as full player characters, just built up enough to handle combat. This is for ease of generation & use.

Make a Social NPC (primary, secondary, some skills), and add an Evasion, a Limit. If relevant, add an Armour value. 

A fragile person is Evasion 2, Limit 8. A hardened fighter might be Evasion 4, Limit 14. Set what feels right for the character.

Make a note of their default attack/s including stat + skill, and weapon / general intent; such as "Strength d8 + Weaponry 2 (battle-axe, cleave appendages)"

Depending on their importance, add a set of abilities - like Powers but in a sentence for brevity and simplicity when running many of them at once, as GMs have to. Draw from the Powers list as inspiration, or use pre-existing samples from Core Foes. Grunts will have none, squad leaders 1-2, lieutenants and larger creatures 3-4 and bosses 5+.

- **Shielded** - Evasion +2

#### NPC Injuries
Injuries for Combat NPCs are simplified. Keep the highest rank Injury they've taken so far and just use it as a flat malus on all their rolls. 

Tally damage for all Injuries (as normal), until they're at their Limit. When the total meets the Limit, they are down.

Take note of any injury labels you think have tactical meaning to the fight in what this NPC can do, but ignore the others. For example:

- **Movement** - leg, knee, ankle, hip injuries
- **Grip** and weapon use - hand, arm, shoulder injuries
- **Sight** - eye and head injuries
- **Concentration** - head trauma, severe pain

#### Example Combat NPC

> *Checkpoint Guard* - Soldier, indifferent, dark, searching for someone
>
> Doesn't want any more problems, and wants the player characters to go away. Will not let them pass without a permit and doesn't care to be bribed.
>
> Strength d8, Agility d6 | Weaponry 2, Intimidation 1, Search 1
>
> Evasion 3, Limit 10, Armour 1 (leather uniform)
>
> Attack: d8 + 2 (Baton, knock-out ruffians)
>
> Abilities: **Raise the Alarm** - makes a very loud noise, alerting others.

### Full Character NPCs
Lastly, for important recurring characters who appear regularly in the story and combat you always have the option to build them using the full player character creation rules. Refer to character-creation.md for that.

You may wish to convert abilities to equivalent Powers, keep them, or replace them.

## Hordes
Sometimes you need to run large groups of hostile NPCs and tracking each individually is not important to the scene at hand, and particularly cumbersome. Imagine a horde of zombies, a swarm of rats, or a vehicle's crew. So you track the 'Horde': a single combat entity.

### Horde Statblock
A horde has four components:

- **Die** - the horde's core threat die, representing its size, ferocity, and collective danger. This is the die rolled for its attacks.
- **Rank** - a single skill-like descriptor and rank representing what the horde does and how well. A rank of 1 or 2 is a rabble. A rank of 4 or 5 is a disciplined and dangerous force.
- **Evasion** - the Evasion of the Horde is the Stat Average of its Die. As the die steps down, so does the Evasion.
- **Limit** - the Knock Out Limit, the total damage threshold at which the horde collapses. Note step points for total damage where the Die drops one step down the chain. Steps do not need to be evenly distributed; place them where they feel dramatically right.

For example:

> *A Murder of Murderous Crows*
>
> A black and screaming cloud of beaks and rage.
>
> Die d10 | Pecking +2 | Evasion 5 | Limit 15 (steps at 6 and 12)

### Running a Horde
Attack and defend against a horde as you would any other combatant. Run attacks vs Evasion, and total Damage from Injuries Ranks as normal. Note if a step threshold has been met to lower the Horde's Die, and adjust Evasion accordingly. Narrate what that looks like in the fiction; crows peeling away, zombies falling, the mob losing its nerve.

When the Horde is at its Limit, it is 'Down' as normal, but describe what that means depending on the narrative: dispersed, destroyed, fled, or broken. A horde rarely dies to the last individual; it simply stops being a coherent threat.

## Planning & Running Combat

### Determining Enemies

Combat NPCs and Hordes are your tools for building enemies (see above). To choose between them consider the following.

Individual combat NPCs are right for individuals that matter to the scene: named characters, skilled opponents, anyone the players might recognise or remember. Hordes are right when the scene has overwhelming numbers of faceless foes, where individual tracking would be beside the point, or named foes are naturally grouped together (such as in a chase, or athletic competition).

A fight against a rival gang of six quirky individuals should be six individual NPCs. A fight against sixty dire rats is a handful of Hordes, with maybe a named lieutenant Rat King as an individual NPC directing them worth tackling head-on. Any non-identifying mob of 5 or more is worth considering as a Horde.

### Setting the Stakes
Most fights are not about killing everyone. When creating an encounter, know who is on what side, and what each side actually wants.

Establish the enemy's goal, just like goals for Social NPCs. Enemies might want to capture, delay, feed, intimidate, drive off, or protect their own. A guard who wants to raise the alarm will behave completely differently to one ordered to take prisoners.

A fight ends when its stakes are resolved, one way or another. You don't have to run a combat until every combatant on a side is Down. Slide between Combat and non-combat by following the goals of both sides, and the flow of the narrative.

### Running Rounds
Crescens Combat details the steps of a round:

1. The GM declares the intentions and targets of all enemies in the scene: letting Players decide how to act.
2. Players discuss among themselves and declare their Actions: thus determining Engagements
3. Resolve the Engagements in order of whichever players are ready and willing.
4. Resolve un-engaged actions (such as Ranged Attacks).
5. Resolve Movement

As GM, you begin each round by describing what each enemy combatant is about to do; "the sorcerer aims a fire bolt at Carolinus", "the dirt bike gang (horde) race for the goal", etc.

Your other main role is to help determine Engagements, and then resolve them with whichever players are ready to go. Use Opposed Agility Rolls (or appropriate) to determine who goes first if speed is a matter of question.

### Resolving Hidden Actions
Certain capable NPCs will be able to use Hidden Actions: enemies who are genuinely deceptive, trained to conceal intent, or whose nature makes them unreadable. Some Hidden Actions will be tied to certain abilities, other powerful NPCs have all their Actions Hidden by default.

When declaring NPC intentions at the start of the Round, don't declare Hidden Actions. State only that the NPC is taking a Hidden Action - and jot down what that is so that it can be properly revealed if a player character takes the Read Action.

If a Hidden Action is part of an Ability, it should say so.

Not all Hidden Action capable NPC actions need to be Hidden either, use it as another tool.

## Progression
Progression in Crescens is narrative driven, as described in the Core mechanics. Players will describe what Stat / Skills / Power they have "In Training", and it's your job to be aware of when that should be rewarded, based on how much character action the player invests in that training. If not already clear, have a brief conversation about what that looks like. The training should be visible at the table, even if only in passing description.

> Viking Harl is training Strength, wanting to get his average d6 to a more impressive d8. As the group adventures, his player describes how, every evening and on quiet days, Harl is finding and hauling, lifting and throwing big rocks, logs and even bystanders. After several in-game weeks of this Sandy, the GM, rewards Harl with a Strength Stat Increase.

You may wish to have several different things In-Training per character, something short, medium and long in scope. Multiple Powers can be in training simultaneously at different intensities, which is encouraged. A character might be slowly building toward a major combat Power over months of play while picking up a Skill Increase after a single intensive downtime.

### Pacing
Pacing is a discussion to have at the beginning as a table. Determine as a group, do you want a break-neck campaign, something slow and steady? Do you want characters to earn progression as individuals, or stick to group-based so-called 'milestone' progression? Is the table happy to have progression almost like loot, or should it always feel hard-earned? The setting and tone of your game will help you all decide this.

## Hazards and Environmental Damage
Not all damage comes from enemies. Falls, fire, poison, extreme cold, crushing pressure, and a hundred other hazards exist depending on the setting. Like any damage, it generates an Injury. Assign the damage as the rank and record an appropriate descriptor. It can then be recovered in the same way.

Ongoing sources of damage, such as burning, might be applied as damage-per-round, but in Crescens guaranteed damage a round will add up quickly. You should probably tie in a Roll to avoid the damage per round.

**Burning** - a TN 6 (adjust depending on the intensity of the flame) Roll or taking d4 damage at the end of every Round. An Action can be used to extinguish the fire for the same TN.

Remember, you can have environmental damage affect combat directly. A room full of smoke from burning could impose a Disadvantage on concentration-heavy actions. A flooded corridor can be Difficult Terrain halving movement. Poisining a foe should grant attackers an Advantage to hit them.

Example Hazards:

| Hazard | Suggested Damage |
|---|---|
| Short fall (one storey) | d4 Bludgeoning |
| Long fall (several storeys) | d4+2 Bludgeoning |
| Terminal-speed fall | d6+4, almost certainly instant death |
| Fire | d4 Burning |
| Conflagration | d8 Burning |
| Poison (mild) | d4 Poison, delayed |
| Poison (severe) | d8 Poison, potentially Fatal |
| Extreme cold | d4 Cold per extended period |
| Crushing / cave-in | d6 to d12 Bludgeoning |
| Vacuum / drowning | d4 per round, escalating |
